Unit 5: The Cuban Revolution(C-NOTES)

Cuban History

Cuba gained its independence from Spain in 1898. Afterward, the United States played a large role in Cuba’s development, owning sugar plantations, hotels, and casinos.

Causes of the Cuban Revolution

  • Poverty: Despite Cuba's wealth from luxury houses, casinos, and Cadillacs, most Cubans lived in poverty.
  • Foreign Control: Many businesses, especially sugar plantations, were owned by Americans.
  • Dictatorship of Batista: Fulgencio Batista's regime allowed only a few elites to benefit from tourism and industry, leaving most Cubans impoverished.

The Cuban Revolution

In the 1950s, Fidel Castro led a movement to overthrow Batista's government. The revolution began in 1956 and succeeded in 1959, placing Castro in power. Initially, Cubans celebrated Batista's removal.

Communism in Cuba

  • Shift to Communism: Castro seized absolute power and declared Cuba a communist state.
  • Suppression of Rights: Citizens lost the right to dissent; many were imprisoned or executed.
  • Land Reforms: Castro nationalized land and property, including American-owned sugar plantations.
  • US Embargo: In response to Cuba's communist shift and land seizures, the United States imposed a trade embargo, which remains in effect today.

Cold War Impact

Cuba allied with the Soviet Union, a communist superpower, during the Cold War. This relationship led to the Cuban Missile Crisis, as Cuba hosted Soviet nuclear weapons, heightening tensions with the United States.

Legacy of the Cuban Revolution

The Cuban Revolution transformed Cuba into a communist state just 90 miles off the coast of Florida. Though it improved access to education and healthcare, the revolution also led to political repression, poverty, and emigration.

Latin American Governments

Students can explain the systems of government used in Brazil, Mexico, and Cuba.

  • Brazil and Mexico: Federal-republican systems where citizens have personal freedoms and voting rights.
  • Cuba: Dictatorship where power is centralized, and personal freedoms are restricted.

Forms of Power:

  • Autocracy: Power held by one individual (e.g., Cuba).
  • Oligarchy: Power held by a few.
  • Democracy: Power held by everyone (e.g., Brazil and Mexico).
