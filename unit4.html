Unit 4: Latin America’s Culture: Language and Religion

Languages of Latin America

  • Spanish: Most of Latin America speaks Spanish due to colonization by Spain.
  • Portuguese: Portuguese is spoken in Brazil because it was colonized by Portugal.
  • Native Languages: Languages like Nahuatl, Mayan, and Quechua were replaced by European languages due to colonization.
  • People in Haiti and French Guiana speak French, as they were colonized by France.

Religion in Latin America

  • Catholicism: The dominant religion, brought by Spanish and Portuguese colonizers, with over 90% of the population identifying as Roman Catholic.
  • Other religions that existed before colonization, like indigenous beliefs, were mostly wiped out or continue to exist in remote areas.

Ethnic Groups in Latin America

  • European Descent: Mainly from Spain and Portugal, Europeans make up a significant portion of the population.
  • Indigenous Peoples: Native populations like the Mayans, Aztecs, and Incas continue to influence the culture.
  • African Descent: Africans were brought to Latin America as slaves, particularly to work on plantations. Many people in Haiti and Jamaica are of African descent.
  • Mestizo: A mix of Native American and European descent. Mestizos make up a large part of the populations in Mexico, Central America, and parts of South America.
  • Mulatto: A mix of African and European ancestry. This is common in places like Cuba and Brazil.

Music of Latin America

Latin American music is vibrant and diverse, reflecting a mixture of European, African, and indigenous influences. Some of the popular styles include:

  • Samba: A mix of African and Portuguese music, originating in Brazil.
  • Tango: A blend of Spanish and Cuban music.
  • Mambo, Rumba, Salsa: These styles are a combination of African and Spanish musical traditions.
