The Treaty of Versailles

After World War I, the Treaty of Versailles placed harsh reparations on Germany, leading to economic depression and setting the stage for further conflict. The treaty included several key restrictions:

  • Responsibility: Germany was forced to accept full responsibility for the war, under the "War Guilt" clause.
  • Reparations: Germany was required to pay huge sums of money to the Allies, which devastated its economy.
  • Land: Germany lost significant territories, which further weakened the country.
  • Military: Severe restrictions were placed on the size and strength of the German military.

The Global Depression

The worldwide depression that followed World War I affected not only Germany but also economies across Europe and the United States:

  • Germany: The Treaty of Versailles caused Germany to plunge into economic depression. The reparations payments led to inflation, unemployment, and widespread poverty.
  • Worldwide Impact: Countries around the world, especially in Europe, struggled with war debts and economic devastation. Inflation, unemployment, and property damage were rampant.
  • United States: The U.S. fell into the Great Depression in 1929 after the stock market crash. Businesses closed, unemployment soared, and many lost their homes and livelihoods.

Businesses globally traded with the U.S., and when the U.S. economy collapsed, it had ripple effects across the world, exacerbating the depression.
