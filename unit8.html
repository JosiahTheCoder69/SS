Unit 8: Canada’s Environment, Economy, and Government

Canada’s Environmental Issues

Learning Target

Students will be able to identify and describe Canada’s three major environmental issues.

Human-Environment Interaction

Humans either **adapt** to their environment or **change** it. Canada faces several major environmental challenges caused by human activity.

1. Pollution of the Great Lakes

  • Caused by **waste dumping, run-off, and acid rain**.
  • Industrial waste, sewage, and chemicals harm aquatic life.
  • Acid rain, caused by coal-burning factories and vehicle emissions, makes water toxic.
  • Pollution leads to deformities in fish, contaminated drinking water, and human health risks.

2. Mining in the Canadian Shield

  • Home to rich deposits of **gold, silver, nickel, and copper**.
  • Mining contaminates **soil and water**, disrupting ecosystems.
  • Causes **human health issues** due to pollution.
  • Short-term economic gain, but long-term environmental damage.

3. Deforestation of Timber

  • Canada’s **Boreal Forest** is over 10,000 years old.
  • Logging for paper and industry destroys animal habitats (e.g., woodland caribou, red fox).
  • Forests are a **limited resource**, and deforestation harms biodiversity.
  • Cutting trees faster than they can regrow leads to **permanent loss**.

Canada’s Economy

Learning Target

Students will be able to answer Canada’s basic economic questions.

Economic System

Canada has a **mixed economy**, closer to a **market economy**. Private businesses make most decisions, but the government controls some industries, like healthcare.

What to Produce?

  • Canada specializes in **energy, agriculture, and manufacturing**.
  • Largest supplier of **energy** to the U.S.
  • One of the **largest wheat and grain producers**.
  • Produces as many **automobiles** as the U.S.

How to Produce?

  • Mostly decided by **businesses and corporations**.
  • Some industries, like **healthcare**, are **government-funded**.

For Whom to Produce?

  • Most food is produced for **Canada**.
  • Most **exports (energy, resources)** go to the **United States**.

Natural Resources

  • Canada has **abundant natural resources**.
  • Major industries include:
    • **Energy:** Oil, natural gas, coal, hydroelectric power.
    • **Agriculture:** Wheat, grain, livestock.
    • **Manufacturing:** Cars, machinery, technology.

Specialization & Diversification

**Over-specialization** can make a country **too dependent** on others. **Diversification** ensures economic stability by producing a variety of goods.

Canada’s Government

Learning Target

Students will be able to explain how Canada’s government works.

Government Type

  • **Parliamentary Democracy** – Citizens elect Members of Parliament.
  • **Constitutional Monarchy** – King/Queen is Head of State but has no real power.
  • **Federal System** – Power is divided between national and provincial governments.

Parliament

Canada’s **legislative branch** is called **Parliament**. It has two houses:

  • House of Commons: Elected representatives.
  • Senate: Members appointed by the Prime Minister.

Prime Minister

The leader of the political party with the most seats in Parliament becomes the **Prime Minister**.

Branches of Government

  • Executive Branch: Prime Minister, Cabinet.
  • Legislative Branch: Parliament (House of Commons & Senate).
  • Judicial Branch: Canada’s court system.

Role of Citizens

  • Citizens **vote for Members of Parliament**.
  • They elect **regional leaders (provincial governors)**.
  • They vote on important issues, like **Quebec’s independence referendums**.

Comparison to the U.S.

Unlike the **Presidential Democracy** in the U.S., where citizens directly elect the President, in Canada, **Parliament selects the Prime Minister**.

Quebec’s Role

  • Many in Quebec identify as **French Canadian**.
  • They held **independence referendums** in **1980** and **1995**.
  • Both failed, but the **1995 vote was very close (49.4% wanted to secede, 50.6% voted to stay).**

Participation & Democracy

Canada’s citizens enjoy **personal freedoms** and **democratic rights**, but participation in elections is key to maintaining democracy.

Quebec Independence Movement

Essential Question

What is the Quebec Independence Movement and how has it affected Canada?

After Independence

Canada received its independence but still had close ties to England, which caused problems. Many French Canadians felt England had too much influence.

Rivalry

French Canadians, mostly in Quebec, have historically felt outnumbered and unheard compared to English Canadians.

French Canadians in WWI

Many French Canadians opposed Canada’s involvement in WWI, believing it only helped England. They felt their opinions were ignored.

Change in Quebec

Quebec was originally a French colony. Over time, more English speakers moved in, making French Canadians fear losing their language and culture.

French Nationalism

Many Quebec residents identified as French Canadians rather than just Canadians. Some wanted Quebec to secede and form its own country.

Quebec Independence Votes

Quebec held independence referendums in 1980 and 1995. Both times, voters chose to stay in Canada, with the 1995 vote being very close (49.4% for secession, 50.6% against).

Quebec’s Status Today

Quebec did not secede but gained equal status within Canada. French remains one of Canada’s official languages.
